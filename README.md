Cross-Race Effect and contact hypothesis analysis

This repository contains the code, analysis, and research materials for my undergraduate dissertation investigating the Cross-Race Effect (CRE) and the contact hypothesis among White individuals in England and Wales.
The project combines quantitative social research methods, statistical analysis in R, and AI-generated facial stimuli to examine racial bias in facial recognition and eyewitness identification.

Research Question

To what extent can the contact hypothesis explain the Cross-Race Effect among White individuals residing in England and Wales?

Objectives
- Test the existence of the Cross-Race Effect (CRE)
- Investigate whether interracial contact improves facial recognition accuracy
- Analyse recognition accuracy across White, Indian, and Black Caribbean facial stimuli
- Evaluate false alarms and response bias in eyewitness-style recognition tasks

Methodology

Experimental Design

- Quantitative deductive research design
- Online facial recognition experiment
- 70 White participants residing in England and Wales
- AI-generated facial stimuli created using Bing Image Creator

Statistical Analysis

Analysis was conducted in R using:

- Descriptive statistics
- d-prime (d') recognition accuracy measures
- ANOVA testing
- Linear regression models
- Data visualisation

Key Findings

- The study found evidence supporting the existence of the Cross-Race Effect.
- White stimuli produced the highest recognition accuracy scores.
- The contact hypothesis was not consistently supported in a multiracial context.
- False alarms significantly influenced recognition performance across racial groups.

Ethical Considerations

AI-generated facial stimuli were used to avoid privacy and consent concerns associated with real participant photographs.
All participant data was anonymised prior to analysis.
